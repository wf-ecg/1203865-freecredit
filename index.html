Federal financial assistance for college is only available for those struggling to make ends meet.

False; Both parents and students often think that federal assistance is available only to those who are struggling financially. As a result, many families bypass the federal financial aid process thinking they earn too much or have too many assets to qualify for federal programs. Regardless of your income, all families should complete the FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) each year. It’s easy to do. If you need extra funding to cover your education costs, there are government and private financing options available.

True; If the funding you receive on your award letter doesn’t cover your total costs, explore other financing options. Your family’s circumstances and preferences will help determine what funding option may fit your situation. As you explore your options, it’s important to:

  • Research all financing programs carefully.
  • Compare eligibility requirements, interest rates, costs and fees, repayment options, and monthly payments of the financing programs you’re considering.
  • Line up a cosigner to help you qualify for a private student loan, which may result in a lower interest rate and quicker credit decision.
  • Seriously consider the amount you plan to borrow so you don’t overextend yourself with too much student loan debt.

Credit history — your financial magic 8-ball

You may start receiving credit card offers when you’re at college. These years can be a good time to practice responsible credit habits and establish your credit history with a card like the Wells Fargo Cash BackSM college card1.

Apply for a credit card in your name, and use it sensibly to make purchases and fund emergencies. As your purchase activity and payment information is reported to the major credit reporting agencies, you’ll start building a solid credit history. Think of this predictor as your financial magic 8-ball, only much more reliable.

Credit basics

Using a credit card means you’re borrowing money that you agree to repay within a certain timeframe. There is a cost to using a credit card called interest, and it’s calculated as a percentage of your balance. There are also fees to consider. You’re responsible for repaying what you borrow plus any fees and the added interest. Unlike a debit or prepaid card, when you responsibly use a credit card in your name, you build a credit history. Credit payment information is reported to major credit bureaus as you make purchases. Missing payments may hurt your credit score. You improve your credit score by staying within your credit limit and paying on time each month.

Credit history is important

You may want to live off campus sometime while in college, get an apartment, or buy a car someday. Before landlords rent to you, they may ask to view your credit report. So can utility companies, lenders, and others. Having good credit gives you added flexibility, more choices, and helps you qualify for better loan rates as you reach these milestones.

How to establish good credit

Fortunately, a roll of the magic 8-ball has nothing to do with establishing good credit, but how you use credit does. Therefore, use credit responsibly, pay your bills on time, and follow these rules to help you stay on track:

  • Borrow only what you can repay.
  • Know your credit limit and stay within it.
  • Make at least the minimum payment due on time every time.
  • Pay off your debt as promised.
  • Avoid cash advances except in an emergency.

Over time, you will establish a credit history that will demonstrate you know how to use credit and are a creditworthy adult.
